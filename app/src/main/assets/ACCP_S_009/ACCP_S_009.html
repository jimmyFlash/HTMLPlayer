Modules summary

Module 4

  • • The long-term goals of asthma management are: Achieving good control of symptoms , maintaining normal activity levels and minimizing future risk of exacerbations, fixed airflow limitation and side-effects.
  • • Effective asthma management relies on three different strategies:
  • • The patient-health care provider partnership enabling asthma patients to gain the knowledge to assume a major role in the management of their asthma improving patient outcomes.
  • • Good communication by health care providers increasing patient satisfaction and adherence.
  • • Health literacy and asthma: The degree to which individuals have the capacity to obtain, process & understand basic health information and services to make appropriate health decisions.
  • • In choosing between treatment options at a population level:
    • - The ‘preferred’ medication at each step is the best treatment for most patients, based on efficacy, effectiveness, safety, Availability and cost at the population level.
    • - Choosing between controller options for individual patients is based on Preferred treatment for symptom control and risk reduction, Patient characteristics and preference as well as practical issues.
  • • Risk factors are factors that increase the patient’s future risk of having exacerbations (flare-ups), loss of lung function, or medication side-effects.
  • • In control-based asthma management, pharmacological and non-pharmacological treatment is adjusted in a continuous cycle that involves assessment, treatment and review.
  • •Patients with asthma should be reviewed regularly.
  • •Stepping up the treatment contains: Day-to-day adjustment, Sustained step up (for at least 2–3 months) and Short-term step up (for 1–2 weeks).
  • •For setting down the treatment: Once good asthma control has been achieved and maintained for 3 months and lung function has reached a plateau, treatment can often be successfully reduced, without loss of asthma control.
  • •Indications for considering referral for expert advice include: Suspected occupational Asthma, Difficulty confirming the diagnosis of asthma, Persistent uncontrolled asthma or frequent exacerbations or Evidence of, risk of, significant treatment side-effects, any risk factors for asthma-related death, Symptoms suggesting complications or sub-types of asthma, as well as additional reasons for referral in children 6–11 years.
